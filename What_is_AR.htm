Augmented Reality (AR) is an interactive experience of a real-world environment where the objects that reside in the real world are enhanced by computer-generated perceptual information.
Origin of AR
Augmented reality (AR) first surfaced in 1968 when Ivan Sutherland, a computer science professional, made an augmented reality display system.

AR makes use of existing reality and tangible objects to produce computer-generated augmentations over the user perception of reality in real time.

For example, Google now allows us to put a 3D model of any animal or other species in our house and we can even take a selfie with it!

This whole concept is quite fascinating. In their book ‘The Fourth Transformation’ Robert Scoble and Shel Isreal say that ten years from today, the centre of our digital lives will no longer be the smart phone, but device that looks like ordinary eyeglasses: except those glasses will have settings for Virtual and Augmented Reality. What you really see and what is computer generated will be mixed so tightly together, that we won’t really be able to tell what is real and what is illusion.

Instead of touching and sliding on a mobile phone, we will make things happen by moving our eyes or by brainwaves. When we talk with someone or play an online game, we will see that person in the same room with us. We will be able to touch and feel her or him through haptic technology. They say that we won’t need to search online with words, because there will be a new Visual Web 100 times larger than the current Internet, and we will find things by images, buy things by brands, or just by looking at a logo on the jacket of a passer-by. Language will be irrelevant, and a merchant in a developing world will have access to global markets. Medical devices will cure schizophrenia, allow quadriplegics to walk. People will be able to touch and feel objects and other people who are not actually there.

From Kindergarten to on-the-job, learning will become experiential. Children will visit great battlefields and tour historic places in VR rather than read about them in text books. Medical students and surgeons will learn and practice on virtual humans rather than cadavers; oil rig workers will understand how to handle emergencies, before the ever leave the home office.

In other words, it will be a whole new world, which sounds promising and fascinating but at the same time very unreal. For the sceptics let us just say that not three decades ago, they could not have imagined a world that we are in today, with no general access to computers, phones and social media. At that time if somebody were to have described these to us it would have sounded even more unreal.
